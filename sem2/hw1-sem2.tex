Suppose $X_1, X_2, \cdots$ are i.i.d. r.v.'s. Find necessary and sufficient conditions on the distribution of $X_1$ in order that 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq1}
\frac{S_n}{n/\log(n)} \rightarrow 0 \text{ wp}1
\end{equation}
\begin{proof}
We claim the condition is that for some $a > 0$.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq2}
E(|X|^{1+a}) < \infty
\end{equation}
Now we show the sufficiency of the claim. Assume \eqref{eq2}. We claim that for sufficiently large $n$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq3}
\frac{n}{\log n} > n^{ \frac{1}{1+ a}}
\end{equation}
To prove that claim, we simplify
\begin{align*}
\frac{n}{\log n} &> n^{ \frac{1}{1+ a}} \\
\frac{n}{n^{ \frac{1}{1+ a}}} &> \log n \\
n^{ \frac{a}{1+ a}} &> \log n \\
\log n &> \frac{1+a}{a}\log \log n
\end{align*}
which we know to be the case. Since by hyptothesis we have \eqref{eq2}, it follows that $E|X| < \infty$. This allows us to assume without loss of generality that $E|X| = 0$. By KMZ SLLN and by \eqref{eq2}, we have
\[
\frac{S_n}{n ^ \frac{1}{1+a}} \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} 0
\]
From \eqref{eq3} we have, for sufficiently large $n$,
\[
\frac{S_n}{\frac{n}{\log n}} < \frac{S_n}{n ^ \frac{1}{1+a}} \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} 0 \text{.}
\]
Since almost sure convergence implies convergence in probability, we have thus proved \eqref{eq1}.

\end{proof}
